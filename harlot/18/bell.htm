A Harlot’s Progress, Plate 1, Detail.

 

Mother Needham welcomes the newly-arrived Moll to London, her touch while seeming a caress, assesses the merchandise.  Needham is placed below this bell. Commentators agree that this denotes the Bell Inn in Wood Street. Paulson also notes a pun on belle, ironically attributed to the bawd.

Other commentary:

Shesgreen: Before the Bell Inn in Wood Street, Mary or Moll Hackabout, newly arrived in London, is caught between the aggressive agents of corrupt, who are set against the crumbling tavern wall, and the ordinary (and passive) middle-class people arranged around the solidly built home (18).

 

Paulson, Life Art and Times: Hogarth’s scenes are constructed of such parallels and interdependencies: the parson and his horse, the girl and her goose, Mother Needham with the sign of the Bell (Belle) over her head (vol. 1 255).

Uglow: The cycle starts with a sweet country girl with a rose in her bosom and her little purse and scissors hanging by her side, arriving off the York wagon at the Bell Inn (the women still inside the wagon, ranged tightly behind the bar, also looklike sheep bound for market) (201).

 

Ireland:The first scene of this domestic tragedy is laid at the Bell Inn, in Wood Street (101).

Paulson Popular and Polite Art:  Hogarth, in A Harlot’s Progress, plate 1, places the sign of a bell against the wall of a tavern so that is appears directly above the head of the bawd Mother Needham (bawds, Addison says . . . ought not violate decorum by operating at the sign of the “Angel”): hanging just over her dowdy head, it suggests the verbal pun belle (33-34).

Lichtenberg:   They have just arrived at the Bell Inn in Wood Street, a well-known public house (6).
