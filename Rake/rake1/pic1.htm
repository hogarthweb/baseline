Rake’s Progress, Plate 1, Detail.

 

The elder Rakewell’s sole work of art consists of a portrait of himself engaged in his favorite activity—counting money.

 

Other commentary:

Shesgreen: The room is without decoration except for a picture of the miser himself who sits with a pair of scales in his hand (a comic reference to the scales of justice) counting money.  Even  in his portrait he wears his hat (now on the mantle beside the candlestick and candlesaver) and overcoat to avoid burning a fire.  Money fearfully secreted in the ceiling spills down on his portrait (28).

Paulson, Hogarth’s Graphic Works: The dead father appears in the portrait over the fireplace (identified by the same fur cap that lies on the mantel), "raking in" gold (161).

Uglow: The portrait above shows Tom’s father “raking” it in (244).

Lichtenberg: sHe lives here in his portrait above the fireplace, and in his dirty deeds throughout the whole room: everything that is going on here, one might say, still moves in him and through him. The portrait is excellently placed, and how subtly has Hogarth indicated by a small touch, which at first seems quite unimportant, that this is in fact a likeness of the old man! (195).
