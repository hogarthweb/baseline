The Four Stages of Cruelty: The Reward of Cruelty, Detail.

 

The setting is Surgeons’ Hall; the President’s chair bears the symbol of the Royal College of Physicians—highlighted by a pointing skeleton on either side of the room.  This one is identified as James Field.

 

Other commentary:

Shesgreen: One figure looks at Tom’s corpse and points to the grinning skeletons of “James Field,” a famous boxer, as if to suggest that Nero’s skeleton will replace Field’s (80). 

Paulson, Hogarth’s Graphic Works: [Nero’s] skeleton will join those of "JAMES FIELD" and "MACLEANE" on display. Field was a pugilist (referred to in PI. 2), hanged February 11, 1751, shortly before Hogarth's prints appeared (February 23). His name must have replaced "GENTL HARRY" as a last-minute change to catch the popular reaction and, tied in with the second plate, to offer another example of cruelty rewarded (214).

Trusler: The skeletons on each side of the print, are inscribed “James Field” (an eminent pugilist), and “Maclean” (a notorious robber).  Both of these worthies died by the rope.  They are pointing to the physician’s crest, which is carved in the upper part of the president’s chair—viz., a hand feeling a pulse; taking a guinea would have been more appropriate to the practice.  The heads of those two heroes of the halter are turned so as to seem ridiculing the president, “scoffing his state, and grinning at his pomp.”  Every countenance in this grisly band is marked with that medical importance which dignifies the professors.  Some of them we discover to be “from Caledonia’s bleak and barren clime” (135).
